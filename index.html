Differential Privacy

What is it?

Differential privacy is a data privacy method that allows data to be shared without revealing unique information about any one individual.[1]

Differential Privacy

Who uses it?

Governments, tech companies, and researchers use it. But anyone with an interest in privacy can use it.

Mechanism

Randomized response adds noise before the answer leaves you.

Sensitive surveys have an evasive-answer problem, so each student flips coins locally before replying [1].

First: maybe tell the truth. Otherwise: take a random route.

The survey collector sees a noisy answer, not the private one.

Chapter 6

Now meet the knob behind the noise.

Remember our gerbil vote? Imagine it wasn’t 27 students — it was 1 million people casting a secret vote for Carl.

In a perfect world, the true count would be stored exactly. But the moment you publish it, anyone can cross-reference it to identify individuals.

Differential Privacy solves this with a single parameter called epsilon (ε) — the “privacy knob.” The right panel shows the true dataset before any noise is applied.

Small ε → more noise → stronger privacy.
Large ε → less noise → more accurate, but more exposed.

At low ε (0.1), the bars jump wildly. At high ε (5.0), they barely change from the truth. Carl’s real count is always hidden inside the noise.

The crowd pattern survives.
No individual is exposed.

This is the same guarantee Apple, Google, and the US Census rely on — just with a much fancier implementation.

K-Anonymity

Meet Sarah

Sarah is 34 years old. She lives in ZIP code 85281. She was just diagnosed with heart disease.

She goes to the hospital for treatment. The hospital records her information in their database.

Age: 34. ZIP: 85281. Condition: Heart Disease.

K-Anonymity

The hospital database is open

The receptionist can see Sarah's exact information. The IT staff can access it. Insurance companies request it for billing.

Anyone with access can search: 'Show me the 34-year-old from ZIP 85281'

Only ONE person matches. It's Sarah. Her medical condition is exposed. This is k=1.

K-Anonymity

What is k-anonymity?

K-anonymity is a privacy technique that groups people together so you can't identify individuals.

The 'k' is a number: if k=5, then at least 5 people share the same characteristics.

Instead of being unique (k=1), you hide in a group of k people. No one can tell which record is yours!

K-Anonymity

How does it work?

The hospital transforms Sarah's exact data into ranges.

Age 34 → Age Range: 30-40

ZIP 85281 → ZIP Area: 852**

Condition stays the same: Heart Disease

K-Anonymity

Now Sarah has company!

With these ranges, Sarah is grouped with 4 other people who also have:

• Age 30-40
• ZIP Area 852**
• Heart Disease

Now when someone searches the database, they find 5 people (k=5). They can't tell which one is Sarah!

K-Anonymity

Try it yourself

Enter your age, ZIP code, and a health concern.

See how you'd be exposed with exact data (k=1), then watch k-anonymity protect you by grouping you with others (k≥5).

K-Anonymity

Why does this matter?

K-anonymity protects privacy while keeping data useful for research.

Doctors can still study 'heart disease in people aged 30-40 in area 852**' for research.

But they can't identify Sarah specifically. She's hidden in a group of 5.

Privacy is protected. Research continues. Win-win! 🎉

K-Anonymity

The tradeoff: choosing k

Higher k = more privacy, but less specific data.

k = 5 (Typical)
Good balance. Sarah is one of 5 people. Age ranges are 10 years (30-40).
k = 100 (Very Private)
Excellent privacy. Sarah is one of 100 people. But age ranges must be very broad (20-60).
k = 2 (Risky)
Weak protection. Sarah is one of only 2 people. Still somewhat identifiable.

K-Anonymity

Real-world impact

K-anonymity is used everywhere to protect sensitive data.

🏥 Healthcare
Hospitals use k-anonymity (k=5 to k=10) to share medical data for research while protecting patient privacy.
📊 Census Data
Government census bureaus use k-anonymity to publish demographic statistics without exposing individuals.
💳 Financial Records
Banks use k-anonymity to analyze spending patterns while protecting customer identities.
